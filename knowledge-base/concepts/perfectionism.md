## Definition
Perfectionism is a personality trait characterized by a compulsive striving for flawless-execution, an insistence on perfection, and a tendency towards self-criticism. It reflects an individual's global belief that mistakes are unacceptable and can be avoided through extreme diligence and efforts. This trait is often intricately linked with [[imposter-syndrome]], where an individual doubts their accomplishments and fears being exposed as a fraud despite evidence of their competence.

## How it works
The concept of perfectionism functions by setting extremely high, if not unattainable, standards for oneself or others. Individuals affected by this condition experience an acute sensitivity to error and inadequacy. These stringent standards can create significant psychological burdens. Striking a balance between setting high goals and understanding the imperfections inherent in human performance is key ([@mogil1998nature; @Hewitt1993; @Frost1990]).

Technically, perfectionism can manifest through several dimensions:
- **Self-oriented**: The individual sets high personal standards for their own work and behavior, with unrealistic expectations.
- **Other-oriented**: The individual imposes high demands or critical evaluations on others, often coupled with strict supervision.
- **Socially prescribed**: The individual perceives that others hold high standards for them, leading to self-imposed pressure.

Perfectionism depends on cognitive schemas and thought patterns, which can be maladaptive when extreme ([@Hewitt1993]). This mindset can lead to cognitive paralysis and analysis paralysis, symptoms where individuals become overwhelmed with the number of choices and options, leading to inaction due to an intense fear of failure or making suboptimal decisions. It is related to issues such as depression and anxiety, often because the unachievable standards lead to chronic self-doubt and internal conflict ([@mogil1998nature; @Frost2000]).

In the context of student-mindset, perfectionism can impede learning and practical application. Students who value perfectionism build a theoretical framework based on the assumption of a linear and flawless progression. They delay practical work in favor of conceptual understanding, akin to the “think before doing” mentality ([@Berkovich1996]). Contrastingly, “engineering-mindset” requires adaptation and a non-linear approach to problem-solving, which is often at odds with perfectionist behavior.

## Variants
- **Healthy perfectionism** extends the concept of perfectionism but is considered adaptive and beneficial when it motivates individuals to produce high-quality work and to be persistent in achieving their goals. Healthy perfectionists also possess a realistic view of the limitations of their abilities and are more flexible in their approaches to problem-solving.
- **Identified patient** in family systems theory is a variant where perfectionism may be seen as a healthy trait in the identified patient, who behaves in a manner that brings a family together, but this can only be effective in the short term, and it can lead to more severe psychological health issues when prolonged.

## Trade-offs
Perfectionism has significant trade-offs associated with it. It can improve performance due to its intense focus on detail and the high standards it sets. However, this comes at the cost of inefficiency and missed opportunities. For instance, the drive for perfection can lead individuals to spend excessive time on a task, delaying its completion, and hindering productivity. This can adversely impact individuals' mental health, creating anxiety and self-doubt, reducing happiness, and impeding growth. The impostor syndrome intensifies these ill effects, making individuals feel inadequate despite their accomplishments, which can further demotivate them and impair their overall performance.
