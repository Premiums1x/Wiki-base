## Retrieval Augmented Generation

### Definition
Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) extends retrieval-enhanced-generation by combining a large pre-trained language model with external knowledge retrieval systems. This approach leverages external text databases or web pages to provide factual grounding to the answers generated by the language model. In RAG, the generation process is split into two stages: a retrieval stage followed by a generation stage. The retrieval stage identifies relevant documents using a search engine, and the retrieved documents are then fed into the generation stage alongside the original question to generate a factually informed response.

### How it Works
In the RAG framework, a query is first transformed into a vector embedding using methods such as transformer-architectures, which are used to represent the semantic meaning of the text in a high-dimensional space. This vector is then searched against a large corpus of documents that have also been transformed into vectors. Retrieval involves searching for the most relevant documents based on the similarity of their vector representations to the query vector. The documents that are deemed the most relevant are then fed into the second stage, the generation stage, where they are used alongside the original text prompt for the final output.

The generation stage is typically a large generative model like GPT, which is fine-tuned to take into account the context provided by the retrieved documents. The model generates a response that is better informed by external factual information. This dual-stage process enables the model to produce responses grounded in real-world data rather than generating content purely based on its internal learned representations, thus reducing the risk of generating false or inconsistent information.

### Variants
RAG comes in different variants based on how retrieval and generation are performed. Some variations may use different mechanisms for retrieval, such as using semantic search with embeddings or keyword-based retrieval. Other versions might integrate retrieval directly into the transformer model, for example by using additional transformer layers that are dedicated to the retrieval task. Implementation generally requires a large corpus of documents for retrieval and a well-optimized search engine or document database setup.

### Trade-offs
The main trade-off of RAG is the increased complexity and computational requirements compared to purely generative models. The retrieval and integration of external information add steps to the process, which can increase inference latency and resource consumption. Additionally, relying on external sources can lead to varying quality and relevance of the information retrieved, impacting the reliability of the generated responses.
